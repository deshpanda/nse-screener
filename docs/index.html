Chapter 1

The promise of easy money

It started the way it starts for everyone: a conversation with a guy who seemed to have it figured out. Trading legends whose students win championships. A chart pattern that reveals where big institutions are secretly buying. An app with "85% accurate" signals. Books that would change everything.

Some of it was real. Some of it was a sales funnel. We decided to find out which was which — but with one twist: instead of trusting anyone, we would rebuild every claim from public data and test it.

Before testing anything clever, you need to know what "doing nothing" earns. In India, parking money in a Nifty index fund via a monthly SIP has historically returned roughly 11–14% a year over long periods. That is the bar. Any strategy that earns less than that is worse than doing nothing.

Chapter 2

The gate: no backtest, no money

We made one deal before writing a line of code: nothing gets real money until it beats the index in a backtest, after costs. Not "looks good on a chart." Beats buy-and-hold Nifty, over years, with every rupee of brokerage and slippage counted.

And because backtests lie easily, we adopted rules that most retail traders never hear about:

The three lie-detectors

1. Out-of-sample testing. Design your strategy on one slice of history (say 2023–2026), then run it once on a slice it has never seen (2017–2022). If it only works on the data you tuned it on, you didn't find an edge — you memorised the past. That's called curve-fitting.

2. Pre-registration. Write down the exact rules, the success criteria, and the test windows before running anything — and commit it publicly (ours are in the repo, timestamped by git). Once you've seen results, it's too late: you'll bend the rules to fit them. Every scientist knows this; almost no trader does.

3. Plateaus, not spikes. A real edge still works if you nudge its settings. If a strategy only works with exactly a 1.5× volume filter and dies at 1.25× or 2×, that setting is a lottery ticket that already won — in the past. You'll see a spectacular live example of this in Chapter 4.

Thirty strategies entered this gauntlet — a thirtieth is pre-registered to run in 2027 (each row's full pre-registered spec and verdict lives as a protocol file in the repo). Here's the scoreboard we'll walk through:

Chapter 3

Before strategy: the data will lie to you

Our very first backtest produced a spectacular result — and it was garbage. Not because the strategy was wrong, but because the data was. If you ever backtest anything, these three traps eat beginners alive:

Trap 1 — stock splits look like crashes. When BSE Ltd gave shareholders a 2:1 bonus, its price went from ₹7,305 to ₹2,358 overnight. Nobody lost a rupee — but raw data shows a -68% "crash". Our backtest dutifully "sold" it at a massive fake loss. Toggle the fix:

Play: the split illusion (real BSE Ltd event, May 2025)
Showing: RAW prices — what a naive backtest sees

We found 479 of these events in our ten-year window — plus 87 more that NSE's official feed doesn't even list, which we had to detect from price ratios. Data cleaning was ~40% of this entire project.

Trap 2 — survivorship bias. If you test on today's stock list, you've quietly excluded every company that crashed and vanished. Your backtest only contains survivors — of course it looks great. We used point-in-time universes everywhere, including the delisted dead.

Trap 3 — lookahead. Using any information before the market actually had it. Sounds obvious; happens constantly. Example: quarterly profits "belong" to March but are announced in May — use them in April and your backtest is psychic. We keyed every fundamental number to its exact announcement timestamp.

Chapter 4 · v1 & v2

The breakout dream, and the curve-fit trap

The internet's favourite strategy: find a strong stock coiling in a tighter and tighter range (the VCP), and buy the moment it breaks out on huge volume, riding the wave of institutional buying.

Anatomy of the setup they sell

Contractions shrink left to right (−25%, −15%, −8%), volume dries up, then the breakout. It's a real, documented pattern. The question isn't whether it exists — it's whether you can make money trading it after costs. That's a different question entirely.

v1 bought strong stocks anywhere near their highs with a fixed 8% stop-loss. Result: -15% while Nifty made +37%. Diagnosis: Indian mid-caps routinely swing 3–4% a day, so an 8% stop checked against every intraday low gets hit by pure noise — half our stops triggered within 5 days. We were systematically selling random dips.

v2 fixed all of that: enter only on the actual breakout day with 1.5× volume, stops scaled to each stock's own volatility (ATR), and a market-health filter. In-sample it beat Nifty by +34 points. We nearly celebrated.

Then we ran the lie-detectors.

That picture is worth memorising. v2's edge lived on a knife-edge: change the volume filter from 1.5× to 1.25× and the edge vanished; to 2× and it collapsed. And out-of-sample, every single variant lost to the index — the in-sample "winner" settings were the worst performers. v2 hadn't discovered how markets work. It had memorised one period's noise. This is what 95% of backtests you see online actually are.

Chapter 5 · v3 & v5

"Just follow the smart money" — the myth that started everything

The original pitch that launched this project: retail always loses, institutions always win, so follow the institutions. India actually publishes two footprints you can follow, and we tested both.

v3 — delivery data. Every day NSE reports what % of trading was taken as delivery. A cluster of unusually-high-delivery up-days should mean quiet institutional accumulation. We tested exactly that, pre-registered. Result: +27.6% vs Nifty's +36.8% — real money made, still worse than doing nothing. The settings-grid was pure scatter: no plateau, no edge.

v5 — bulk deals. When anyone trades over 0.5% of a company in a day, NSE publishes their name the same evening. We collected 151,787 deals over ten years, classified the buyers, kept only "sticky money" (mutual funds, insurers, pension funds — 2,400 conviction buys), and bought whatever they bought, next morning.

Why it failed — the one-day head start you can never have

Result: +18.1% vs Nifty's +36.8%, every variant negative. The mechanism matters more than the number: by the time a footprint is public, the market has repriced it. Disclosure comes at 7 PM; you buy at 9:15 AM the next day into a price that already knows. The visible part of "smart money" is already in the price — the invisible part is the only part that pays, and by definition you can't see it.

Bonus discovery: the most frequent bulk-deal names weren't legendary investors at all — they were high-frequency trading firms appearing on BOTH sides of the same stock on the same day. What looks like "smart money activity" in an app is mostly machines churning.

Chapter 6 · v6

"But this rally is OBVIOUS" — the guaranteed-profit test

The Author was sure of this one — his words at the time: "When Trump praised Dell, I bought the same day — +80% in a month. Micron with the AI wave — +60%. These are guaranteed. No such stock ever went nowhere." Instead of arguing, we built the test.

Feel the pull of that logic. Now watch what happens when you're forced to count every case instead of the ones memory kept. We defined an "obvious rally" mechanically — an S&P 500 stock jumping ≥7% in a day on 3× normal volume, already in an uptrend (exactly the Dell/Micron shape) — and enumerated all 927 such events over ten years, using the index membership as it existed each day. Chapter 7 · v4

The one that survived: boring, monthly momentum

After all the clever ideas died, the survivor turned out to be the least glamorous thing imaginable — a strategy with decades of academic evidence called cross-sectional momentum:

The complete rulebook (yes, this is all of it)

① On the last trading day of each month, rank every liquid NSE stock by its return over the past 12 months excluding the most recent month (recent winners briefly reverse — so skip them).
② Buy the top 20, equal amounts in each.
③ Next month, re-rank. Sell what dropped off the list, buy what entered. Typically ~6 changes.
④ The circuit-breaker: if Nifty is below its own 200-day average at month-end, sell everything and sit in cash until it recovers.

Twelve decisions a year. No screens to watch, no patterns to spot, no tips to buy.

Why does this survive when everything sexier died? Because it isn't exploiting a chart squiggle — it's exploiting human behaviour: investors underreact to good news, anchor to old prices, and sell winners too early (the exact instinct you feel in your own portfolio). Behaviour doesn't get patched like a software bug. And notice — it passed every lie-detector the others failed: positive out-of-sample, a settings plateau (Chapter 4's chart, second button), profits spread across dozens of stocks and years.

What two more weeks of auditing added (details in the audit box and Season 2): merging renamed tickers' histories — a pure data-fidelity fix — revealed the never-seen-years edge was understated (+116 points, not +77); a statistical penalty for every strategy we ever tried still leaves the edge ~99.5% likely real; every vanished-stock event was audited by name; and the derivatives experiment accidentally mapped the strategy's habitat — its alpha lives in the mid-caps BELOW the futures market's attention, not the crowded large caps. Ten challengers have tried to take its title. All ten failed.

Where it stands now: passing the backtest earns a strategy paper trading, not money — three months of live signals logged publicly in the repo before a single rupee moves. Its very first live instruction, July 2026: "market below the 200-day line — hold cash." The first thing the winning strategy ever did was refuse to trade. Sit with that.

Chapter 7½ · v8

Epilogue: we tried the winner in America. It died.

If momentum is human nature, it should work everywhere — so we ran v4's exact recipe on ten years of US data (S&P 500, membership as it existed each day). Out-of-sample, every variant lost to simply holding the index, by 40 to 100 points. The same rules that work in India lost badly in New York.

Two lessons worth the price. First, edges are local: India's market is retail-heavy and slow to reprice; US large caps are the most institutionally arbitraged assets on earth — naive momentum there was crowded out years ago. Second, the market-health circuit-breaker that saves v4 in India actively hurt in the US, whose crashes V-recover so fast that stepping aside means missing the rebound. You cannot copy-paste a strategy across markets any more than you can copy-paste it from a YouTube video.

This also quietly answers "isn't the US market easier?" — the question that started this whole project. With identical discipline: no. Harder.

Chapter 7¾ · v9–v12

The smart-money autopsy — and the law of dying edges

"Follow the smart money" deserved a full trial, so we gave it one at every disclosure speed the law allows: India's same-evening bulk deals (v5), US insiders' 2-day Form 4 filings (v9), famous hedge funds' 45-day 13F filings (v10), and US senators' 45-day STOCK Act disclosures (v11). Four bodies:

FootprintYou learn about it afterVerdict
India bulk/block dealssame eveningdead — repriced by next open
US insider purchases2 daysdead since ~2023 (see below)
Hedge-fund 13F holdingsup to 45 daysdead for your wallet — see the audit box for the twist
US Senate trades~45 daysdead — never worked at all

Late addendum — the one that nearly lived. After this chapter was written we tested the last untried smart-money feed: Indian promoters buying their own companies' stock (disclosed within days). It was the strongest signal of the entire hunt — the only one to beat the fair-comparison baseline in both eras. It still failed: the profits are lottery-shaped, a few big winners hiding a typical trade that earns nothing, and we require the TYPICAL trade to work. An early cousin finding — "downgraded stocks lose 14.6%" — looked like a keeper, until we parsed all 1,043 actual downgrade letters from their PDFs: the effect vanished. The tiny first sample had caught only the loudest disasters. We print the correction as prominently as the claim, because that is the whole deal.

The senator data held a beautiful trap worth naming: 68% of their followed trades "made money" — in a market where everything made money. Against the index: a coin flip. Subscription services sell you the first number and hope you never compute the second.

The most important discovery of the whole project

Three of these tests showed the same time signature, in independent datasets: insider-cluster drift, hedge-fund-position diffusion, and India's buy-the-news-pop drift were all genuinely profitable in 2016–2022 — and all measurably dead in 2023–2026. What changed? Free tracking apps, zero-commission brokers, and a million retail algorithms arrived. Edges don't just exist or not exist — they get eaten, and the more visible the signal, the faster the meal. If you learned a trick from a video, an app, or a friend's hot streak, you are looking at the fossil of an edge. The market that paid it is gone.

This also answered a question one of us kept asking: "what about buying the obvious news pop and selling a day or two later?" Tested at every entry and exit combination a retail trader can actually execute (v12): in the US, pops fade — the chasers are the exit liquidity. In India it truly worked once — +0.8% net per event, until 2023. Then everyone got the same idea.

Chapter 8

The actual edge is the method

Strip away the code and ten years of data, and what survived is a checklist. Any strategy anyone ever pitches you — a guru, an app, your own clever idea at 2 AM — walk it through these six questions:

  • Compared to doing nothing? Not "did it profit" — did it beat a Nifty SIP after costs? (v3 and v5 made money and still failed this.)
  • Tested on unseen data? If it was tuned and judged on the same years, it's memorisation. (v2's +34 became −64.)
  • Rules written before results? If criteria moved after seeing numbers, the test is theatre. (Our protocols are git-timestamped.)
  • Plateau or knife-edge? Nudge every setting ±25%. A real edge degrades gracefully; a lottery ticket dies instantly.
  • Would the losers' version look the same? For every Dell there were dozens of forgotten non-Dells. Demand the full enumeration, not the highlight reel.
  • Does the seller profit whether you win or lose? Courses, signals, subscriptions — the surest edge in markets is selling shovels.

The audit — checking our own referee

After thirteen verdicts, the Author asked the right question: "are our constraints and intervals even correct to judge?" A full re-audit followed, and it changed things — which is exactly what should happen when someone checks the referee:

One benchmark was unfair. In 2023–26 the average S&P 500 stock lost to the index by ~2% a quarter (a handful of giants carried everything), so "beat SPY" was nearly impossible for any stock-picker. Re-tested against the average-stock null: insiders still showed zero skill — but the famous hedge funds genuinely pick above-average stocks (+3.6 points). Their skill is real; indexing beat skill itself. The verdict for your wallet stands, the insult to the gurus is retracted.

One question was asked wrongly. Low-volatility stocks (v13) failed as a standalone strategy — but a defensive asset should be judged inside a portfolio. A 70/30 momentum/low-vol blend beat pure momentum on risk-adjusted return in both eras and cut the worst drawdown from -33.5% to -23.8%. Because we found this after peeking at the data, it earns only a paper-trial spot, not instant belief. Honesty has rules.

One more knob, one more corpse — and a lesson about decimals. The Author asked whether the monthly review was sacred: "it's not compulsory to hold a stock for a month, right?" Right — so we swept the review cadence from weekly to quarterly. Weekly checking turned the champion into a corpse (-82 points out-of-sample: the 12-month signal barely moves in a week, so frequent review just buys trading costs and whipsaw — the quant version of checking your portfolio too often). Quarterly naps through crashes. Monthly sits on the plateau. And the humbling bonus: the SAME monthly cadence anchored to different calendar days swung the in-sample result by ±70 points — pure date luck. That is why no verdict here ever rested on one number. A single backtest figure is an estimate; only patterns across windows, variants and anchors count.

And the survivor got audited hardest. v4's backtest assumed vanished stocks (delistings) exit at their last price. Under a hostile 50%-loss assumption its out-of-sample pass would FLIP to a fail — so we audited all 24 vanishings by name: 7 renames, 16 temporary exchange-surveillance moves that returned ~3 months later at an average -5%, one merger. Real cost: ~5 points, not 141. The pass stands on verified events, not assumptions. If anyone shows you a backtest, ask what happens to stocks that disappear from it. The answer is usually "nothing good," and rarely volunteered.

"It was never my thinking that made the big money. It was my sitting." — the century-old line from Reminiscences of a Stock Operator that our data keeps re-proving.

This project continues — the paper log updates monthly, an earnings-momentum test (v7) is running as this page is written, and every protocol, verdict and line of code lives in the open repo. The failures will stay on this page permanently. They're the point.

Chapter 11 · v24, the stranger's algorithm

The algorithm a stranger built — and what our gauntlet said

The Author follows a builder online. Browsing his work one evening, there it was: a momentum algorithm — rank the 200 largest NSE stocks by their trailing year, hold the top 20, rebalance monthly, and one clever wrinkle: keep any stock you already own until it falls out of the top 40, a hysteresis band that stops borderline names being churned every month. No backtest was published with it. We gave it the full treatment: pre-registered, tested verbatim on our panel, both eras, real costs.

The result deserves respect: it beat the index in both eras — only the second strategy of twenty-six to do so, in a graveyard full of professional-sounding ideas that couldn't. A stranger on the internet, no course to sell, built something real. But the champion beat it everywhere: v4's extra rules — skip the most recent month, fish in the wider liquid pond — where, as the derivatives test later proved, the smaller half is where the alpha actually lives, and step to cash in sick markets — were worth roughly ten percentage points a year and two-thirds of the drawdown.

Two takeaways. The obvious one: even a genuinely good strategy isn't the best available strategy, and the difference compounds. The generous one: his hysteresis band demonstrably helped — without it his out-of-sample edge vanished entirely (+23 → −4). That idea now sits in our queue as a future refinement for v4 itself, to be tested on data that doesn't exist yet. Good ideas get honored here; they just don't get believed for free.

Chapter 9 · v7, the final challenger

The last idea standing, and why the champion kept its title

One hypothesis outranked all others on paper: earnings momentum — pick from the strongest stocks the ones whose actual quarterly profits are accelerating. It's the half of the classic playbooks nobody retail ever tests, because it needs something almost nobody has: ten years of quarterly results stamped with the exact moment each number became public. We built that dataset from 43,421 regulatory filings — including fixing a one-word difference in how banks report profit that silently broke a fifth of the market's data.

The result was the closest fight of the project. In the design years, earnings-filtered momentum beat plain momentum on every measure. In the held-out years, plain momentum won just as clearly. One window each — and our pre-committed rule for exactly this case says: the incumbent keeps the title. A tie broken by discretion after seeing results isn't a tie; it's a leak.

The consolation finding is real, though: earnings-filtered momentum beat the index in both windows, across every settings variant. The information in earnings is genuine. It just doesn't add anything that price momentum hadn't already noticed — by the time profits accelerate, the price usually knows.

Final score: thirty strategies, twenty-nine deaths, one survivor — boring monthly momentum with a circuit-breaker, now proving itself in a public paper trial before a single rupee moves. The twenty-nine failures were not wasted work — they are the reason the one survivor can be trusted.

The experiment we refused to fake — and what we're doing instead

One idea remains genuinely promising: avoid companies whose founders have pledged their own shares — a documented fragility with a real mechanism. The problem: the exchange only publishes today's pledge levels. Testing today's data against the past would smuggle in hindsight — the worst pledge disasters are exactly the companies that no longer exist to be tested. Most backtest sellers would run it anyway. We refused.

Instead, the machine now records the full pledge snapshot every month, permanently. In a year, we will own a point-in-time pledge history that exists nowhere else for free — and then the experiment runs honestly, on data we grew ourselves. Some datasets can't be downloaded. They have to be farmed.

Postscript — sterner tests, same verdict. After the Author asked whether our judging itself was sound, the gate gained three new instruments from the professional literature: rolling-window consistency, a penalty for how many strategies we tried (the deflated Sharpe ratio), and a measure of whether our own selection process overfits. Verdicts under the sterner judge: the survivor's edge is ~99.5% likely to be real even after paying a statistical penalty for every attempt we had made — and, printed here so nobody can pretend otherwise later: one in four two-year stretches loses to the index, once by 50 points. Somewhere ahead there are two years where this strategy will look broken and won't be. Whether we sit through them is not a math question.

Postscript 2 — auditing the machine itself. One doubt outranks all others: what if our backtesting engine is subtly broken, and every verdict on this page inherits the bug? So we pointed the exact engine at thirty years of US data and checked it against the most-replicated result in finance — the momentum portfolios maintained by Ken French's data library, built by a completely independent pipeline from different raw data. Month by month over 353 months, our engine's portfolio moved with the academic benchmark at a correlation of 0.89, and reproduced the documented plot beat for beat: the premium era before 2009, the famous momentum crash of 2009 (−26 points in one year), and the post-2010 decay (pass criteria frozen in advance ↗, code ↗). The machine that declared twenty-nine strategies dead and one alive measures reality the way five decades of published research does. The verdicts stand on tested ground.

Postscript 3 — the visitor every backtest forgets: the tax office. Every edge number on this page is pre-tax, like every backtest you will ever be shown. But the survivor's monthly churn means almost every gain it realizes is short-term (of 308 completed round-trips in the design years, the number held past twelve months was zero), taxed at 20% — while its opponent, buy-and-hold Nifty, pays long-term tax of 12.5%, once, years later. We rebuilt the whole backtest as a tax ledger — every lot, every fiscal year, loss offsets and carry-forwards per the rules (method frozen first ↗, code ↗). The verdict: the tax drag is ~6 points a year — worse than the 2–5 we predicted in the protocol, and we print that miss — yet the edge survives it in both eras: +54 points after tax in the design years, +63 in the never-seen years. The lesson generalizes: any strategy whose pre-tax edge is under ~6 points a year is, for an Indian investor, a more stressful way to own an index fund. Several corpses on this page died twice.
